The verb SER — "to be" (permanent facts)

Spanish has two verbs for "to be". Today we learn ser, used for identity, origin, and permanent qualities.

PronounSerEnglish
yosoyI am

él / ellaeshe / she is
nosotros/assomoswe are
vosotros/assoisyou all are (Spain)
ellos / ellassonthey are
Key insight: Spanish often drops the pronoun because the verb ending tells you who's speaking. Soy española = I am Spanish — no need to say yo.

SER vs ESTAR in one line:
Soy cansada — weird (you're permanently tired?)
Estoy cansada ✓ — I'm (currently) tired.
Soy española ✓ — I'm Spanish (identity).
Estoy en Madrid ✓ — I'm in Madrid (location).

Grammar

Telling time — ¿Qué hora es?

Spanish tells time with ser. One o'clock uses singular, the rest use plural:

SpanishEnglish
Es la una.It's one o'clock.
Son las dos.It's two o'clock.
Son las tres y media.It's half past three.
Son las cuatro y cuarto.It's quarter past four.
Son las cinco menos cuarto.It's quarter to five.
Pattern: Son las [number] y [minutes]
After 30 min you "subtract" from the next hour: menos = minus/to.

Grammar

All nouns have a gender

In Spanish, every noun is either masculine or feminine. The article changes to match:

TypeDefinite (the)Indefinite (a/an)
Masculineel libroun libro
Femininela casauna casa
Masc. plurallos librosunos libros
Fem. plurallas casasunas casas
Shortcut rules (not absolute, but ~80% reliable):
Words ending in -o → usually masculine: el libro, el año
Words ending in -a → usually feminine: la casa, la mesa
Exceptions exist: el día (day) is masculine despite ending in -a!

Grammar

Regular -ar verbs in present tense

Most Spanish verbs end in -ar, -er, or -ir. Today we tackle -ar — the most common group. Take the verb, drop -ar, add these endings:

PronounEndingHablar (to speak)
yo-ohablo
tú-ashablas
él/ella-ahabla
nosotros-amoshablamos
ellos-anhablan
Same pattern for: trabajar (to work), caminar (to walk), escuchar (to listen), cocinar (to cook), estudiar (to study).
Drop -ar → add ending. That's it.
