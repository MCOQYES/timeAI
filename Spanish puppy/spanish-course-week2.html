Grammar

The other two verb families

Last week you learned -ar verbs. Spanish has two more groups. The endings are very similar — the main difference is the vowel that changes from a to e.

Pronoun-er: comer (eat)-ir: vivir (live)
yocomovivo
túcomesvives
él / ellacomevive
nosotroscomemosvivimos
elloscomenviven
Pattern shortcut: -er and -ir are almost identical. The only difference is nosotros: comemos vs vivimos. Everything else is the same endings.

Grammar

HAY — the most useful word in Spanish

Hay comes from haber and means both "there is" and "there are". It never changes — one form for everything.

SpanishEnglish
Hay un gato.There is a cat.
Hay muchos gatos.There are many cats.
¿Hay agua?Is there any water?
No hay problema.There's no problem.
¿Cuántos hay?How many are there?
Important: After hay, use the indefinite article (un/una/unos/unas) or no article at all — never el/la.
✓ Hay un restaurante.   ✗ Hay el restaurante.

Grammar

Possessive adjectives — my, your, his, her…

These are the words that show ownership. In Spanish they agree with the thing owned, not the owner.

EnglishSingularPlural
mymimis
your (informal)tutus
his / her / your (formal)susus
ournuestro/anuestros/as
theirsusus
Watch out: mi (my) has no accent. mí (with accent) means "me". They sound the same but mean different things.
Also: tu = your (no accent). tú (with accent) = you.

Grammar

GUSTAR — the backwards verb

Gustar works differently from English "to like". In Spanish, the thing you like is the subject — it "pleases" you. The person who likes is the indirect object.

SpanishLiteralEnglish meaning
Me gusta el café.Coffee pleases me.I like coffee.
Me gustan los gatos.Cats please me.I like cats.
Te gusta bailar.Dancing pleases you.You like to dance.
Le gusta la música.Music pleases him/her.He/she likes music.
Nos gusta viajar.Travelling pleases us.We like to travel.
The key rule: Use gusta (singular) when what you like is singular or is a verb. Use gustan (plural) when what you like is plural.
Me gusta el libro. → Me gustan los libros.

Grammar

IR + A + infinitive — talking about plans

This is the easiest way to talk about the future. You don't need to learn new verb forms — just conjugate ir (to go) and add a + any infinitive verb.

IR (conjugated) + a + infinitive verb
PronounIRExample
yovoyVoy a comer.
túvas¿Vas a estudiar?
él / ellavaElla va a viajar.
nosotrosvamosVamos a salir.
ellosvanVan a llegar tarde.
¡Vamos! — when vamos a is followed by an infinitive it means "we are going to...". But by itself, ¡Vamos! means "Let's go!" — you already learned this on Day 7.

Full dialogue — read it, then try without looking

A rich conversation using 2 weeks of Spanish

— ¿Cómo eres tú? Descríbete.
What are you like? Describe yourself.
— Soy morena, tengo los ojos oscuros. Soy simpática y bastante divertida.
I'm dark-haired, I have dark eyes. I'm friendly and quite fun.
— ¿Y qué te gusta hacer?
And what do you like to do?
— Me encanta viajar y me gusta mucho leer. No me gusta madrugar.
I love travelling and I really like reading. I don't like getting up early.
— ¿Tienes familia aquí en España?
Do you have family here in Spain?
— No, mi familia vive en Mongolia. Pero mi novio es español.
No, my family lives in Mongolia. But my boyfriend is Spanish.
— ¿Y qué vais a hacer este fin de semana?
And what are you going to do this weekend?
— Vamos a comer con sus amigos. Hay un restaurante muy bueno cerca.
We're going to eat with his friends. There's a very good restaurant nearby.
