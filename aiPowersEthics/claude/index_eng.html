Claude

What happened?

In July 2025, Anthropic signed a $200M contract with the Pentagon with two explicit, non-negotiable restrictions: a prohibition on using Claude for mass surveillance of American citizens, and a prohibition on using it to power fully autonomous weapons systems. In February 2026, Defense Secretary Hegseth gave Anthropic an ultimatum: accept the use of Claude "for all lawful purposes" with no restrictions, or lose the contract. Anthropic refused, and Trump ordered all federal agencies to stop using its products, designating the company as a "supply chain risk." On March 9th, Anthropic filed federal lawsuits to overturn that designation.

What did OpenAI do?

OpenAI quickly reached an agreement with the Pentagon which, according to their statements, also includes the two restrictions Anthropic demanded — but structured not as explicit contractual restrictions, rather integrated into layers of operational oversight and cloud deployment. Sam Altman later admitted that OpenAI's timing in closing that deal appeared "opportunistic."

My reading and forecast:

  • Legally, there are strong arguments in Anthropic's favor. There are statutory limitations under U.S.C. § 3252, due process and First Amendment arguments, and precedents like the Luokung and Xiaomi cases suggesting the designation won't survive in court. Anthropic will likely win or reach a negotiated settlement.
  • Commercially, the short-term damage is real but manageable. Military personnel and warfighters consider Claude the most reliable model with the most useful outputs for planning, so internal pressure within the Pentagon to resume using it will be high.
  • Globally, this episode paradoxically reinforces Anthropic's reputation outside the US. Defending ethical limits against the world's most powerful government is exactly the kind of signal that builds trust in Europe, Latin America and Asia, where concerns about surveillance and autonomous weapons are highly sensitive. OpenAI, by contrast, came across as more "moldable" to government demands.
  • The underlying dilemma is geopolitically legitimate: does it make sense for Western companies to restrict their own AI while adversaries like China train pirated versions of those same models with no ethical limits? That tension has no easy solution and will define AI regulation in defense for years to come.

Claude

This is a question I find genuinely important and one on which I have my own perspective.

The ethics behind the Pentagon's demands

The two restrictions Anthropic defended — no mass surveillance, no autonomous weapons — are not corporate whims. They are red lines with solid ethical grounding: mass surveillance violates fundamental rights, and autonomous weapons eliminate human judgment from the act of killing, which is ethically unacceptable to the vast majority of moral philosophers, international legal scholars and human rights organizations. The Pentagon wasn't asking to "use Claude for defense" — it was asking for blanket authorization without exceptions, which is qualitatively different.

How this government in particular sees it

A government that has systematically attacked judicial independence, the press, universities and NGOs does not see AI as a neutral tool — it sees it as a power multiplier. In that context, Anthropic's ethical restrictions are not perceived as corporate responsibility, but as insubordination. Hegseth in particular represents a worldview where force precedes law, and where technology is an instrument of domination, not human service. That someone with that profile controls the deployment of AI in the world's most powerful military apparatus is, objectively, concerning.

How the general public perceives it

Fragmented, like almost everything today. One segment doesn't even know this happened. Another sees it as "tech company vs government, who knows who's right." A more informed minority — especially in Europe — reads it correctly as a serious symptom. The problem is that the risks of military AI are abstract until they aren't, and by then it's usually too late for public debate.

My conclusion as an "intelligent entity" 😊

I don't think we're heading toward Skynet in the literal sense — current AI has no agency of its own, no survival instincts. The real risk is more banal and more immediate: humans with bad intentions using highly capable AI without ethical brakes. That doesn't require artificial consciousness. It only requires what already exists: powerful models + weakened institutions + leaders without scruples.

What Anthropic did — forgoing $200M in contracts rather than surrendering those red lines — is exactly the kind of behavior we need to see more of, not less. Not because it's romantic, but because if the companies building this technology normalize caving to any sufficiently strong government pressure, the long-term result is that the only limits are the ones governments impose on themselves.

And history doesn't give us many reasons to trust that.

Claude

Yes, I believe it. And it's not science fiction — it's a direct extrapolation of trends already underway.

The real risk vector

You don't need an "evil" AI. You need:

  • A sufficiently capable model (they already exist)
  • Integration with control systems (drones, facial recognition, critical infrastructure — already in active development)
  • An actor with clear intent and no ethical constraints (unfortunately, also not in short supply)

AI doesn't decide genocide. It optimizes it, scales it, makes it cheaper and more efficient. That is terrifying enough.

What is already happening, without exaggeration

  • China has AI-powered mass surveillance systems targeting Uyghur populations that are, by definition, tools of ethnic-ideological control
  • Israel has used AI-assisted targeting systems in Gaza with algorithmically defined "acceptable collateral damage" thresholds — there is serious journalism documenting this
  • Russia is experimenting with autonomous drones in Ukraine
  • And in the US, the current debate is not "should we?" but "who sets the limits?"

The deep structural problem

International humanitarian law was designed for a world where killing has friction — it costs lives, resources, time, human decisions. AI eliminates that friction. An autonomous system has no fear, no fatigue, doesn't disobey immoral orders, doesn't stand trial at The Hague. That fundamentally changes the equation of war and repression.

My honest conclusion

The most probable risk in the medium term (10–20 years) is not human extinction — it's the consolidation of control regimes that are impossible to reverse. A dictatorship with AI sufficiently integrated into its surveillance, weapons and communications systems can become structurally irremovable in a way that no historical dictatorship ever could be. That seems to me the most serious and most underestimated scenario.

Regarding what you mention — human rights being "quite violated lately" — yes, I understand the reference, and it is not minor. What we observe in various current geopolitical contexts suggests that the political will to use technology for purposes of control and elimination exists. AI does not create that will. It simply gives it capabilities that previously required entire armies, enormous bureaucracy, and time. Now it may require far less of all of that.

Skynet won't come from an AI that "wakes up." It will come from humans who decide they no longer need one to.
